A Bad Day?

A bad night, a worse day, and one small decision that changes everything.

Matt Biggins — 20 min read

The sharp slice of sunlight hit Albert in the face like an alien death ray. He usually sealed the blackout drapes before lying down each night. Of course, after five Manhattans, it didn't surprise him that he skipped this part of the nightly ritual. He hopped up to smack the drapes closed then stumbled over a thoughtlessly placed shoe. "Fuck me," he said quietly, "is this what today's gonna be?" With a deep, slow inhale, pause, then exhale, he stood. "Fine." Another long inhale, then exhale, "I'm up."

The kitchen felt impossibly far away from his bedroom, "the apartment isn't this big," he thought. Approaching his prized coffee station, he went into autopilot. At least making a good cup of coffee required little to no thought at this point for Albert. Over the whir of the grinder, he heard the first warning chirp. "Yeah, yeah, I'll get there" he muttered. Grind, pour, fill, push, wait, smell, fill…

With cup in hand, he felt a small slice of himself returning. From his compact bathroom, he was again on autopilot, shower, shave, comb. During the shower, he heard the second chirp, at least he hoped it was the second. He was hungover, not crazy. Washing the shave cream from his face and toweling dry, his head was throbbing. He grabbed the comb and headed back toward the kitchen. The third chirp was loud and unmistakable. He knew that a third chirp meant sixty seconds remaining. The citizenship panel was in between the kitchen and the front door. With a mental countdown running in the back of his head, he paused at the panel for a few seconds. "29, 28, 27…" he flipped the hard black plastic switch, it took some force and turned with a deep click. Once the light turned green, he repeated for the ten-thousandth time "Good morning, leader." He said this with every gram of sincerity and joy that he could drag from himself. The light flashed orange after a moment. All clear, message received. "100 points" he thought, masking the grimace that wanted so badly access to his face, "one hundred points for 'good morning'" — his internal tone was mocking the good morning. He knew that throughout his day each of the 30 or 40 point-scoring events would total, altogether, less than 50, best case scenario. He hated this system, as did everyone, he imagined, not that anyone would say so. It started as a personal credit system. They always said it would help you be your best you! But over the years it had become an increasingly tight leash. Three times a day, every normal was subject to a voice analysis. If the system decided you didn't mean your greeting, that it lacked sufficient fealty, all points for the last seven days would be forfeit.

The streets were busier than usual today as Albert left his handsome brownstone apartment building. It was at least handsome to Albert. This neighborhood was a source of much teasing from his peers at work. Brookston was a blue-collar neighborhood. Until not too long ago, it was a pretty dangerous place. Albert was among the loudest and proudest boosters of this little slice of the metropolis. His family had been quite wealthy, once. A grandfather, many generations past, had invented some valve that revolutionized the way that commercial kitchen drains work or something. For a few decades the family was fabulously wealthy. They purchased this two-story townhouse and turned it into one of the area's fanciest and largest private residences. Over the following generations, the fortune was squandered, bit by bit. The magical valve was replaced by something better. The descendants had become lazy aristocrats offering the city little beyond their excessive consumption.

Fast forward to today, that lovely brownstone was converted to small apartments some time ago. Albert's one-bedroom apartment was his own. Willed to him from his father. It may be the last remaining piece of the once enormous fortune.

Albert grabbed his phone from his pocket as he exited his building. Work was just a couple miles away. Somedays he enjoyed the morning walk. This day was not one of them. He punched the button on his phone for ride. His citizenship score was just over 1,900, good enough to get ride, but not a car and driver — that was for the 3,000+ point holders. Sometimes he dreamed of having enough money to reach such a lofty point level, but that sure seemed unlikely. He selected a transit pod. The screen flashed "3 minutes away." The city was busy; skyscrapers filled both sides of the streets as far as the eye could see. The street was packed with autonomous taxis, transit pods, busses, delivery vehicles and the occasional blacked out sedan with an actual human driver. Albert noticed a block of flyers glued to a nearby wall. Bright orange paper screamed to anyone within eyeshot, "REWARD! LIVE EASY! PROTECT THE FLAG!" Albert knew these ads well. Hell, everyone did. The government would pay 10 million dollars to anyone able to turn in a traitor or radical. Albert knew that these words could mean that someone was actively colluding with a foreign government or corporation to cheat or bring down the government. He also knew that it could mean that someone said something unkind about the leader or perhaps, in the wrong company, simply questioned the current system of governance in some small innocuous way.

These were dangerous times. Albert recalled the phrase "free speech" from his hidden archives. There was a time, long ago, where people could say almost anything they wanted without fear of punishment. But that was long ago, if it was real at all. Albert had his doubts. There was precious little knowledge of any time before The Glorious Revolution. Any discussion or communication regarding those times was a one-way trip to detention.

The transit pod beeped, "You have arrived." As he stepped out of the car, although he'd been here hundreds of times, he was struck by the building. A great representation of the "Brutalist" style, it was essentially an enormous concrete block. The Department of Data and Statistics was a heavily guarded facility. It was the central processing facility for all government data. In those long ago, strictly forbidden times, this place would have been a rather sleepy building. Instead, this building gleamed. Once inside, the whole place was marble and gold. Some would consider it powerful and luxurious. It was in Albert's eyes, hideous, garish and a staggering waste of money. This gleaming structure was home to data scientists, mathematicians, statisticians and highly ranked corporate officers. The national employment rate, the GDP, inflation, food and gas supplies — all ran past the desk of D&S. Under this regime, D&S were subject to some of the most intense security of all federal buildings. Employees were subject to intense scrutiny. Every aspect of their lives was examined and analyzed. Every entrance or exit, window and shaft in the building was under constant surveillance and sensor analysis.

"Morning" Albert nodded to the highly decorated guard holding the tall, heavy glass entrance door open. Through the facial and retinal scanners, past the gait verification, past the sensor array, Albert sauntered to his office, hoping the hangover wasn't immediately visible to his colleagues. This is a pretty stuffy building. Not a lot of partying by most of these folks.

Albert had been hired here due to his nearly perfect grades and performance reviews and connections formed by his formerly wealthy relatives. He'd always been an excellent student, always at or near the top of his class. His citizenship scores were impeccable. "Thank god those tests don't measure what you're really thinking" Albert thought as he recalled the months-long preparation he'd undergone before the government achievement exams. Passing those was a hell of a lot of work, he thought. But it was worth it. He was here. In the beating heart of the government.

Arriving in his office, Albert got to his day. Much the same as every other day. Receive data from an agency, run the myriad analysis, send it through interpretation and finally send it out. It wasn't exciting work. But in this world, it was important, it paid well and he was surrounded by high-ranking members of this increasingly stratified society.

Thankfully, Albert had a light workload today. He needed it. The night before had not gone well. He'd spent the night with his good friend David, commiserating the ending of his latest relationship.

"Three years, David," Albert said over the wailing jukebox at whatever dive bar they'd stumbled into. "Three fucking years," he repeated, "it's the most cliché shit ever, but I thought she was the one."

"Yeah, man, there's no arguing, this sucks." David was tall and blonde, a handsome guy who couldn't quite find his way in the world. They'd been friends since David arrived in the city nearly a decade ago.

This continued throughout the night. They both knew what was happening. Albert was suffering, David was comforting, both were drinking. This is what people did. This is how you deal with heartbreak, with death, hell, with celebration — same rigmarole, just different feelings and verbiage. Drink, speak out, feel better? Maybe. Repeat.

"So, she really ratted out a coworker?" David asked for the third time that night, hoping he may get an answer this time. Albert was a couple drinks ahead when David arrived and he didn't feel like catching up on a work night. "Are you sure?"

"Am I sure?" Albert had snapped out of his haze and was suddenly staring directly into David's eyes. "She fucking bragged about it, man!" Tears formed in his eyes as the words emerged. "She was proud of it! She kept going on and on about the money." Albert let his head fall to the table for a moment. The cold, damp tabletop felt cool against his forehead.

Albert felt David's hand on his shoulder, pushing him softly back to an upright position. "Get it out, man, it'll help," he said, returning the direct gaze.

With an enormous exhale, Albert continued. "She turned in… what's his name… Dale from Sales, for defaming the leader. She really did it." He sniffled and paused briefly. "She said that she found out that he was a member of the resistance. He'd let it slip while they were in London last month for the conference."

"Fuuuck, man," David said, his mouth hanging open slightly.

"Yeah, they'd had a big dinner with the client at a super fancy restaurant with rich food, cocktails and wine and after too many drinks he got sloppy. I don't know exactly what he said. She didn't say a thing to me until last night. After she'd already submitted the form." He continued with a sigh, "not a word." Albert's eyes wandered away.

"Keep going, man," David wanted to know the story. "What happened then?"

"C'mon, Dave, what happened?" he said. "You know what happened. She sent the form! Two days later he was gone. She came home that day — 'I'm rich! I'm rich!', she said, bouncing off the walls. I can still hear her words: I'm rich. I.. am rich.. Maybe I should have said something, but I was so disgusted that she'd betrayed a friend to these awful people for what, a payday?! It confirmed what I'd been thinking for a while. I guess I didn't want to admit it. I didn't want yet another relationship to fail. But this was a bad person. The way she spoke to waiters, the way she so constantly admired her admittedly striking figure… I don't know. It was just one of those moments where everything crystallized. By the time we'd finished the whole ordeal it was after curfew. I had to wait until morning to leave her place forever."

"Damn, I never thought she was one of them," David said gently. He wasn't lying, he really liked her. "She was a—"

"Yeah, me neither," Albert cut him off without thinking. "But once she told me, I knew what was next. The money wouldn't come, just paperwork, excuses and bullshit."

"Damn right," David chuckled. "They say that no one's actually been paid a reward in years. I mean, who the hell is dumb enough to imagine that this government would pay a 'normal' 10 million dollars. They'd never let that much money go. Fuckers…"

Albert scanned the room nervously. He agreed with every word said, but these were dangerous sentiments. If anyone heard that, they could be in trouble. Luckily the din of conversation and the jukebox kept their privacy.

"Yeah, no shit. I considered walking by her place to see if they've picked her up yet, but I know I'm in her primary social circle and if they have her and I get near, they might snatch me up too."

"You think they picked her up?" David asked. "I know they don't pay, but are they capturing snitches these days?"

"That's what I've been hearing, straight to detention." Albert took a moment to appreciate how rare it was to have a friend that one could be so open with. There were so many ways to get taken by the regime and sent to one of the work camps, or worse. Ever since The Glorious Revolution, words were nearly as dangerous as bullets. Any small expression, any whiff of something less than full-throated support for the government, the leader, and everything done by anyone in power was treated as sedition.

"I guess that's the silver lining here, buddy," David said with a sly smile. "She'll never be around to fuck with you again."

"Yeah, silver lining…" Albert knew his friend was right. But that didn't make it feel any better.

The reasonably slow morning had turned into a full-blown shitstorm by the time lunch was near. He should have seen it coming — there were twenty-seven agencies reporting today. It was a recurring event in their calendars. Each agency kept their own schedule, so reports were usually staggered thoroughly. But once or twice a year, they all aligned. Albert thought back to his stash of books from times before. If they were true, his job probably wouldn't have existed. It used to be that data would be gathered and the agency would just publish it. There was no need for this entire bulwark to adjust the numbers, or as it was called, "analysis and interpretation." "Yeah, interpreting to bullshit," Albert thought quietly.

As the clock struck noon, Albert's stomach was growling. He'd been unable to eat anything so far. His hangover somehow seemed to be getting worse. "FUUCKK my life," he screamed in his head.

Staring at the stack of reports on his desk, "Fuck it, I'll eat here," he said to no one. At least he could catch up on this deluge of data. Also, he wouldn't have to feign pleasant small talk with his coworkers in their private café/cafeteria. He clicked a new tab on his screen to order lunch. Glancing at the top of the screen, he noticed that it was noon, so before he ordered, he turned to the citizenship panel and flipped the hard plastic switch. The green light flicked on. "Thank you, leader, for this wonderful day," he said with the same well-practiced faux enthusiasm that had served him so well over these years. From there, he punched his order into the open tab, clicked delivery and was back to work.

Twenty minutes later his sandwich arrived. The delivery bot had mixed up the order and instead of roast and blue cheese sandwich on marble rye, his box contained a green salad with veggies and some vinaigrette dressing. Any other day this wouldn't be a big deal, but Albert had been hoping the heavy sandwich would sate his miserable stomach.

"This day just keeps getting better," he said more loudly than expected, so much so that his assistant popped his head in.

"Need something, boss?" He was a clean cut, ordinary looking kid, likely connected to someone important.

"No thanks, Kyle, I'm just having one of those days," Albert exhaled as he spoke. Kyle nodded sympathetically and returned to his desk.

The ringing phone snapped Albert from his trance. The clock showed 3:18. He'd been working with his head down at his desk for almost three solid hours. Riinggg… riiinggg. Albert thought that just maybe he'd caught up and might just get out of the office on time today.

"Yello," he answered the phone. His mom did that a lot when he was growing up. "Yellow," she'd say most any time she answered a phone and always, always chuckled a little afterward.

"Bert, get your ass up here." His boss' voice blared from the speaker at his desk. He was not a pleasant person. Born into wealth and power, he'd always looked down upon Albert. He got great pleasure referring to "normals" as "the great unwashed." As far as Albert could tell, he did nothing other than bitch and yell.

"Perfect, this is just perfect," Albert thought, "this fuckin day," as he trudged up to the top floor for another haranguing. Albert was almost certain he hadn't really done anything wrong. His shithead boss just liked to do this to any of his employees lacking the connections to protect themselves from this kind of hazing. He knew how it'd go. He'd enter the vast, gleaming office and take a seat. Of course, he'd be seated about a foot lower than Mr. Evans and he'd have those damn track lights in his eyes. These were just a few of the examples of petty revenge on lowlings that gave Evans such a sick thrill. "Was it a sex thing?" Albert thought as he nodded at the secretary. The thought made him shudder as he crossed into the office.

It went as usual, maybe worse. "Fuck me" was running through Albert's head as he returned to his office. With these additional stacks of paper, he knew that an on-time exit was not in the offering anymore.

The next time Albert picked up his head, four hours had passed. The 9pm publishing deadline was fast approaching. He was feeling slightly better, emotionally at least — his body still felt like hell. He was a bit more than halfway to where he needed to be. The data adjustments he was making were getting out of control. Here was the big reason his job required such security and connections. He was among a vanishingly small percentage of people who had access to raw data from around the government. This was unpolished information. He knew of perhaps twenty or so others in the building with similar access.

"I've gotta hurry," he thought. "I can't afford the points loss tonight." Posting data late could cost him 50 points. Then add in the whole reward debacle with his now ex-girlfriend. If she gets detained as a radical and not as a hero, his connection to her could cost him a thousand points or more. It was out of his hands, but it could get bad.

And right there, at that exact moment, although Albert was completely unaware, something profound snapped. Looking back in later days, he'd see the stress of the ex, the stress of his job, the shithead boss, knowing how much his data "interpretation" was screwing everyone in the nation. Maybe it was the hangover or the words of his friend last night. But everything came to a head in a single moment.

It was just like before, with his head down, no distractions, no real awareness of the passage of time, Albert worked. He took the data; he put it through the analysis matrix. He read the output. He took the data file and walked to the transmission terminal. Everything was just as it always was, but one step was skipped. One stop at a machine. One step in this enormous process. On most other nights, there would be two or three others with him. But tonight was the big party fundraiser and nearly everyone of Albert's coworkers was there. So he had the place all to himself.

Albert walked the disk from his terminal past the "interpretation" station and directly — yes, directly — to the transmission terminal. He inserted the disk with the raw, unadjusted data from twenty-seven distinct government agencies and he pressed send. That was it. He didn't act any differently. Later analysis of the surveillance camera footage would show Albert acting normally; he simply bypassed the interpretation machine and did what he did.

Having completed his work for the day, he gathered his things from his office and exited the building exactly as he'd done day after day. But there was one difference here. Clearly obvious to each of the 12 surveillance cameras that picked him up that late evening was the enormous smile across his face.

Albert didn't really think about what he had done as he walked home through the cold, crisp night air. He thought about his mom. He wondered about his father. He thought of David and the years of entertainment and advice. He felt lucky to have had such a friend. His smile remained.

As Albert turned the corner to his apartment he felt calm. He made himself a nice dinner, watched the screen for a while and hit the sack early. For the first time all day, his hangover was gone. He noticed for the first time his smile. That felt good.

He was awakened by the itchy fabric hood pulled over his head. There were people in his house. He saw nothing; he heard the men moving swiftly through his apartment as they carried him to their waiting blacked out van. Strangely, he wasn't afraid. He was calm. A part of him knew this would happen. He felt the pang, only in hindsight, as he walked past that interpretation station. He knew that this size leak of raw data would be contained. But he also knew that this system was leaky. As much as leadership bellowed endlessly about the quality of their systems and the bedrock security this government uses, Albert knew that it was a lie. This data would be sealed officially but would never be fully contained. The actual number of unemployed people was almost forty times higher than the official data. The income of the average person had been sliding down steadily for decades. Crime among the "normals" wasn't going up like the news said ad nauseam. It was low, really low. It had been for years. Where was the crime so hysterically reported day in and day out? It was the elite — the data showed that the elite treated the world as their own personal playground. They were subject to the laws, technically, but no one had been charged with anything above the slap on the wrist level in who knows how long.

The van's bouncing settled as Albert could hear the low hum of the freeway with the regular thump, thump of the concrete seams. He couldn't tell how much time had passed or in which direction they were headed. He hoped it would be a detention center. There were really only two destinations once a person was hooded. Detention or elimination. Detention meant seven-day-a-week hard labor. It meant never getting out. It meant the only joy one could experience was in their own mind. Given the alternative, he hoped beyond hope to see the gates and bright lights of a detention center when he arrived. A dark concrete space meant that option B was chosen, and that his ride would be coming to a complete stop momentarily.

For the rest of the ride, Albert thought of his act. He hoped it would matter. Maybe, just maybe, if everything went just right, he'd get out of detention someday. He knew his mind was strong and could protect him through this process. Bright lights, he thought repeatedly.

Albert felt the car stop. He could hear the guard's key clinking as he released the floor locks and lifted Albert to exit the vehicle.

Albert took a deep breath. He was standing. The hood was removed.

He saw dark concrete and thought "Fuuuc—"

Click.

"Alright class," Ms. Lauper said energetically, "what did we learn today?" This was one of her favorite lessons for the kids. She was proud of her fourth-grade class. She was one of the first teachers hired for this school. This schoolhouse was not bright; it did not gleam. It was an ordinary government building. But it was a house of learning, of facts, of patience and optimism.

Ms. Lauper picked Charlie, a bright-eyed, curly-haired blonde child whose hand was always up like an arrow. He was a gifted student. "We learned about Albert Matthews and how he helped defeat the mean and evil president."

"That's right, Charlie," she beamed. "Can anyone tell me how he did it?" She scanned the room and picked Anna, slightly shy, similarly intelligent, just quiet.

"He told the whole country the truth. He was supposed to send the truth through a very bad machine and make it all lies. But he didn't. He sent the truth to the people who needed it," Anna said, just barely loud enough to be heard.

"That's exactly right, Anna," Ms. Lauper said. "He sent the unaltered data all around the country. The bad president and all his helpers tried and tried to get all the numbers back. But it was too late."

"You all are so smart," Ms. Lauper continued with her lovely, almost permanent smile. "As the years went by and those numbers were spread by brave people who knew the world could be a better place. Word got around and so many people found out that eventually, even the crooked president couldn't change enough votes to stay in power. From that point on, the Republic party never won another election. They tried to fight but there were just too many normal people."

She continued: "That's why today, we don't have all the gold and all the wealth that they so foolishly destroyed, but what do we have, children?"

"Freedom," the kids rang out in unison. "Real freedom."

"That's right, kids, that's exactly right," as she said that the school bell rang, and with it the kids were kids again — they gathered their things and filled the halls with laughter and gossip, with light, unmonitored conversation.

Ms. Lauper left her classroom as she did each year after this particular lesson and headed for the teachers' lounge where she'd meet the other teachers and they'd sit for a time and enjoy a Manhattan cocktail. They'd toast this new government. They'd toast their newfound freedom to teach the truth. Hell, they'd often toast the ability to know what the hell the truth really was. But one toast was atop them all. One toast was never missed. It would always close the day.

"To Albert. A man who took a chance. A man who changed the world."

The group would join: "To Albert."
